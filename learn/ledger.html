The Ledger

Understanding how transactions are recorded and tracked in accounting

Back to Learn

What is a Ledger?

A ledger is a book or collection of accounts where transactions are recorded. In today's accounting systems, ledgers are typically computerized, but the concept remains the same: to organize financial information by account.

Types of Ledgers

Ledger Type Description
General Ledger The main accounting record containing all accounts used by the business
Subsidiary Ledgers Detailed records supporting specific general ledger accounts
Sales Ledger Records all sales made on credit and tracks customer balances (Trade Receivables)
Purchase Ledger Records all purchases made on credit and tracks supplier balances (Trade Payables)
Cash Book Records all cash receipts and payments

The Accounting Cycle

The ledger is a central component in the accounting cycle:

1. Transactions

Business events with financial impact

2. Journal Entries

Record debits and credits for each transaction

3. Posting

Transfer information to ledger accounts

4. Trial Balance

List all accounts with their balances

5. Financial Statements

Prepare reports from ledger information

Journalizing vs. Posting

These are two distinct but related processes in recording transactions:

Journalizing Posting
Recording transactions in a journal Transferring journal entries to the ledger
Chronological order Organized by account
First step in recording Second step in recording
Book of original entry Book of final entry

Trial Balance

After all transactions are posted to the ledger, a trial balance is prepared to ensure that the total debits equal the total credits.

Simple Trial Balance as of June 30, 2025:

Account Debit Credit
Cash £8,000
Trade Receivables £12,000
Equipment £25,000
Trade Payables £7,500
Loans Payable £15,000
Owner's Capital £20,000
Service Revenue £18,000

Utilities Expense £2,000
Wages Expense £10,000
Total £60,500 £60,500

Modern Ledgers

Today's computerized accounting systems automatically:

  • Post journal entries to the appropriate ledger accounts
  • Calculate account balances in real-time
  • Generate trial balances on demand
  • Produce financial statements directly from ledger data
